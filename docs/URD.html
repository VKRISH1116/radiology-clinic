1Purpose

This document captures the user requirements for a web application that lets a radiology / diagnostic clinic in India run appointments, patient registration, report delivery, and referring-doctor payout tracking online — replacing today's manual, phone-based process. It describes what users need, not how the system will be built. It is the input to the Product Requirements Document (PRD, Phase 2) and the design (Phase 3).

2Business context & objectives

The problem today. Appointments are booked by phone; patient details and consent are collected on paper; reports are collected in person; and referring-doctor payouts are tracked manually — slow, error-prone, and hard to audit.

Business objectives (the "why" behind the app):

  • O1 — Reduce phone/paper workload for front-desk staff.
  • O2 — Give patients self-service booking and secure, 24/7 access to their reports.
  • O3 — Make referral payouts accurate, transparent, and auditable.
  • O4 — Protect sensitive patient data with explicit consent and controlled access.

3Scope

In scope (MVP)

Patient registration & login · appointment booking · consent capture · appointment status · secure report download · staff daily list & walk-in registration · report upload · status updates · billed-amount capture · admin dashboard · referring-doctor management · configurable referral rules · payout tracking.

Out of scope (for now)

Full PACS / DICOM medical-image viewing · insurance claims & billing automation · online payment collection · multi-clinic / multi-location support · a login portal for referring doctors.

Deferred to backlog (consciously postponed in review, not dropped): patients editing their own saved profile / contact details after registration.

Why draw a scope line at all? An MVP ("Minimum Viable Product") delivers the smallest set of features that is genuinely useful, so you can ship, learn, and iterate — rather than trying to build everything and finishing nothing. Everything above the line is what we commit to; everything below is explicitly deferred.

6Non-functional requirements

These describe qualities of the system — how well it must behave — as opposed to features. They're easy to forget and expensive to retrofit, so we name them now.

IDQualityRequirement
NFR-01Security / access controlReports and records are visible only to the owning patient and authorised staff. Access is role-based.
NFR-02Privacy & consentExplicit consent is captured and stored (with timestamp) before personal data is collected. Aligns with India's DPDP Act 2023 spirit.
NFR-03AuditabilityKey actions (report upload, report access, payout changes) are logged for accountability.
NFR-04UsabilityUsable by non-technical patients and front-desk staff with minimal training; works on a typical phone browser.
NFR-05Reliability / availabilityAvailable during clinic operating hours; no data loss on reports once uploaded.
NFR-06Data integrityReferral calculations are accurate and reproducible from the stored rules and billed amounts.
NFR-07PerformanceCommon screens (daily list, dashboard, report download) respond within a few seconds under normal load.
NFR-08Booking integrityThe system prevents booking more appointments into a slot than its defined capacity — no double-booking. (added in review)

7Constraints & assumptions

Constraints (fixed, non-negotiable)

  • C1 — Technology: Backend Java + Spring Boot, Frontend React, Database PostgreSQL.
  • C2 — Deployment: Runs on localhost during development; AWS is the long-term target.
  • C3 — Single clinic: One location; no multi-tenant support in the MVP.
  • C4 — Learning project: Built incrementally, one SDLC phase at a time, prioritising understanding.

Assumptions (believed true; to confirm)

  • AS1 — Reports are produced outside the system (e.g. by a radiologist) and uploaded as files (e.g. PDF).
  • AS2 — Referring doctors do not need to log in for the MVP; staff/admin manage their data.
  • AS3 — Payment is collected offline; the system only records the billed amount, it doesn't process payments.
  • AS4 — The primary language is English for the MVP.
  • AS5 — One appointment may include multiple ultrasound studies; the billed amount is the sum of their catalogue prices.

8Resolved questions (elicitation outcomes)

The draft's open questions have been resolved with the clinic. Recorded here for traceability — ✓ = decided, ○ = deferred/assumed.

OQ-01 ✓ Scan types: Ultrasound-only clinic. Nine studies at fixed prices — see Appendix A. Obstetrics is a category containing sub-studies (NT, TIFFA, Growth & Doppler).
OQ-02 ✓ Patient login: Email + password (no SMS/OTP dependency for the MVP).
OQ-03 ✓ Referral model: A configurable rule engine seeded with a default rule of 20% of the final bill. When multiple rules match, the most specific wins (doctor+scan-type > doctor > scan-type > amount-range > default). Reconciles the brief's "variable rate" intent with today's flat 20%.
OQ-04 ○ Report-ready notifications (SMS/email): Deferred out of the MVP (remains UR-P-07 = Could).
OQ-05 ✓ Studies per appointment: Multiple studies allowed per appointment; billed amount = sum of the studies' prices.
OQ-06 ○ Account provisioning: Assumption — a seeded first admin creates staff and other admin accounts (UR-A-07).

9Glossary

URD — User Requirements Document
This document: what users need, in the problem space.
PRD — Product Requirements Document
Next phase: translates these needs into product features, MVP boundary, and acceptance criteria.
NFR — Non-Functional Requirement
A quality the system must have (security, performance, usability) rather than a feature.
MVP — Minimum Viable Product
The smallest genuinely useful version we can ship and learn from.
MoSCoW
Prioritisation scheme: Must / Should / Could / Won't-have (now).
Referral rule
How a referring doctor's payout is computed — a percentage of the billed amount that can vary by doctor, scan type, or amount range.
Visit
A patient's attendance for a scan; carries the billed amount, referring doctor, status, and report.
PACS / DICOM
Standard systems/formats for storing and viewing medical images — explicitly out of scope.
Consent
The patient's explicit, recorded agreement to collect and process their data.
